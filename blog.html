Gentle reflections

The comfort blanket of food

By Sarah Reeve · Change Mind and Body Therapy

When people think about emotional eating, they often think about a lack of willpower. Someone who “just can’t stop eating," who “needs more discipline.” Someone who “knows what to do but won’t do it.”

But both through my work as a therapist and through lived experience, I have learned that emotional eating is rarely about food itself. More often, it is about regulation.

Food can become a comfort blanket.

Not in a dramatic or conscious way. Most people do not wake up one day and decide to use food as emotional support. Instead, it develops quietly over time. Food becomes something reliable, soothing, predictable and is always available.

And when we begin to understand that, shame starts to soften.

What is a comfort blanket?

Many of us remember having something as a child that brought comfort. Maybe a blanket, a teddy or a favourite toy. Something that helped us feel calm, safe and reassured.

These are often known as “comfort objects” or “transitional objects”, a concept first explored by Donald Winnicott, who recognised how important these objects can be in helping children manage separation, anxiety and emotional development.

A comfort object becomes more than just an item. It represents safety, consistency and connection. It helps regulate overwhelming feelings.

For some children, this process naturally fades as they develop emotional regulation skills and internal feelings of safety.

But for others, emotional regulation may not be consistently taught or modelled. Some children grow up learning to suppress feelings, avoid conflict, manage other people’s emotions or cope alone. Others experience environments where emotional needs are minimised, dismissed or simply unsupported.

In those situations, it makes sense that something external may begin to take on the role of comfort and regulation. And for many people, that “something” becomes food.

Why food works

Food is not comforting purely because it tastes nice. It soothes the nervous system.

From the very beginning of life, feeding is linked to warmth, fullness, closeness and settling. A baby feeds, feels physically soothed and often falls asleep. That connection between eating and comfort does not disappear in adulthood.

When we eat, the parasympathetic nervous system — often known as the “rest and digest” system — becomes activated: chewing, swallowing, warmth and the feeling of fullness. All of these send signals of safety through the body.

There is also a sensory component. The crunch of toast. The warmth of soup. The melting softness of chocolate. Food can become rhythmic, repetitive and predictable, which helps lower the nervous system’s sense of threat.

At the same time, foods high in sugar, fat and salt can stimulate dopamine and serotonin pathways associated with reward, relief and pleasure.

For a moment, anxiety softens. The emotional sting reduces. The body settles. And that matters. Because many people who emotionally eat are not simply “craving food”. They are trying to regulate distress.

The cycle of restriction and shame

Unfortunately, many people who struggle with emotional eating eventually enter dieting cycles. This is where things often become more complicated.

Restriction creates internal conflict.

“I want to eat.” “I shouldn’t eat.” “If I eat this, I’ve failed.”

That internal battle increases anxiety and emotional arousal. Eventually, the urge to regulate becomes stronger than the urge to restrict.

And once permission to eat is finally given, relief often arrives almost instantly. Many people describe this as “giving in”, but psychologically and physiologically, something important is happening.

The internal conflict has ended.

The nervous system settles because certainty has replaced conflict. This is one of the reasons emotional eating can feel so powerful. Not because somebody is weak. But because the behaviour genuinely regulates something.

The difficulty is that shame quickly follows. Diet culture often adds morality onto food: “Good foods.” “Bad foods.” “Being good.” “Off track.”

Over time, eating stops being just eating. It becomes connected to identity, worth, control and failure. Many clients I work with are not just battling food. They are battling guilt, fear, self-criticism and years of judgement.

When food becomes the enemy

One of the saddest parts of disordered eating is that food often stops being enjoyable. It becomes emotionally loaded.

People begin analysing every choice. Thinking ahead constantly. Judging themselves while eating. Feeling watched in public. Eating secretly in private.

Food becomes both the solution and the problem... The thing that soothes distress… and the thing that creates more shame.

I often describe this as an “internal war”. And in my experience, healing rarely comes from escalating that war. It comes from reducing it.

Learning to sit with emotions

One of the hardest things I had to learn personally — and one of the hardest things clients often learn in therapy — is how to sit with emotion without immediately trying to escape it.

That sounds simple. But it can feel terrifying. Especially for people who have spent years using food to avoid, numb or regulate difficult feelings.

At first, emotions can feel overwhelming, raw, painful, even urgent. But emotions are not dangerous. And when we pause long enough to notice them, rather than immediately reacting to them, something important happens:

We create space. Space to think. Space to regulate. Space to respond differently.

This does not mean somebody can never emotionally eat again. In fact, one of the most healing shifts can come from removing the moral panic around food.

For some people, the first step is not saying, “You must never comfort eat again.” The first step is learning to acknowledge: “I need comfort right now.” Without shame, punishment, or turning it into failure.

Because once the internal dialogue softens, the emotional intensity often softens too.

Compassion changes the conversation

One of the biggest shifts I experienced personally was learning to challenge my own internal critical voice. So many people struggling with emotional eating speak to themselves in ways they would never speak to another human being.

“You’ve failed.” “You’ve ruined everything.” “You’re weak.”

But healing rarely grows from self-hatred. Compassion changes the conversation. When we begin responding to ourselves with curiosity instead of punishment, we start asking different questions:

What am I feeling right now? What do I actually need? What is this food helping me cope with?

Those questions create understanding, and understanding creates space for change.

Final thoughts

Emotional eating is not simply about food. It is often about comfort, safety, regulation and attachment. For many people, food became a way of coping long before they realised that was what they were doing.

And while emotional eating can absolutely become distressing and harmful, understanding the purpose behind the behaviour is often where healing begins. Not through shame, punishment or escalating the internal war. But through compassion, understanding and learning, slowly, how to regulate without fear.

Because sometimes the question is not: “Why can’t I stop eating?” Sometimes the real question is: “What has food been helping me survive?”
